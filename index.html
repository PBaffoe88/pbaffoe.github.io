The Childcare Constraint

Estimating the causal effect of childcare access on labor supply in Central Minnesota

Research Question

Does access to licensed childcare increase labor-force participation, employment, and full-time work among parents of young children?

Economic Contribution

This project links two decades of Minnesota childcare licensing records with ACS PUMS microdata and labor-market indicators to evaluate how childcare supply and subsidy eligibility shape parental labor-supply decisions.

Key Findings

  • Responses differ sharply by household type: single mothers exhibit a significant negative labor-force participation response (β ≈ −0.14), while single fathers exhibit a significant positive employment response (β ≈ +0.14) in higher-supply areas.
  • Full-time employment falls sharply at the CCAP subsidy-eligibility threshold (RD estimate ≈ −0.30, p = 0.001), while labor-force participation and employment show no statistically meaningful discontinuity.
  • The results suggest that the design and phaseout of childcare assistance may matter as much as the overall level of childcare supply.
  • RD estimates are interpreted with bandwidth-sensitivity caveats, and placebo cutoffs show no comparable discontinuity.

Data: 20 years of Minnesota DHS/DCYF licensing records, ACS PUMS (N ≈ 103,000), Minnesota DEED, QCEW, and FRED.

Buffer Depletion and Price Instability

The inventory channel of monetary policy in U.S. manufacturing

Research Question

Do interest-rate shocks affect inventory behavior and producer-price volatility in inventory-intensive manufacturing sectors?

Economic Contribution

This study evaluates inventories as a monetary-policy transmission mechanism. It tests whether higher financing costs and weaker demand cause shipments to contract faster than inventories, producing temporary buffer accumulation and greater price instability.

Key Findings

  • After a contractionary monetary shock, inventory-intensive sectors show a significant, persistent increase in inventory-to-shipments ratios, peaking near +0.4 and remaining elevated for roughly 18 months.
  • Producer-price volatility rises more in inventory-intensive sectors and peaks about six months after the shock.
  • A 12-month placebo shock produces no comparable response, strengthening the causal interpretation.
  • Split-sample evidence indicates that sector inventory intensity materially changes the response to monetary tightening.

Data: Census M3/FRED, BLS PPI/FRED, Federal Reserve speeches, inflation expectations, and San Francisco Fed monetary-policy surprises.

Oil Prices and Geopolitical Risk

What 35 years of data say about crude oil and geopolitical uncertainty

Research Question

Does geopolitical uncertainty generate persistent oil-price increases, or does it primarily affect volatility and market regimes?

Economic Contribution

The project combines regime-switching models, local projections, volatility models, and momentum indicators to distinguish price-level effects from uncertainty-driven volatility.

Key Findings

  • A two-state Markov-switching model identifies a distinct high-volatility crisis regime, with probabilities rising around the 1990 Gulf War, the global financial crisis, 2014–2016, the 2020 pandemic shock, and 2022.
  • A geopolitical-risk shock is followed by a modest cumulative decline in WTI returns of approximately 0.6% over the following month.
  • The evidence indicates that geopolitical risk manifests more strongly through volatility and regime switching than through persistent oil-price increases.
  • Momentum indicators reveal substantial differences across one-, three-, six-, and twelve-month lookback windows.

Data: Caldara–Iacoviello Geopolitical Risk Index, WTI spot prices, and macroeconomic indicators, 1988–2026.

M.S. thesis — St. Cloud State University

Spillover Effects of U.S. Fiscal Policy on OECD Monetary Policy

How U.S. fiscal shocks transmit to central-bank decisions in more than 30 economies

Research Question

How do expansionary U.S. fiscal-policy shocks influence short-term policy rates across OECD economies?

Key Findings

  • Monetary-policy responses diverge systematically across country groups.
  • High-income OECD economies tend to respond with expansionary monetary policy.
  • Other OECD economies are more likely to tighten policy when facing the same U.S. fiscal shock.
  • The empirical design identifies U.S. structural shocks first and then traces country-level dynamic responses.

Data: OECD Statistics, FRED, and national accounts.
