Working paper — preparing for submission

The Childcare Constraint

Estimating the causal effect of childcare access on labor supply in Central Minnesota

Research Question

Does access to licensed childcare increase labor-force participation, employment, and full-time work among parents of young children?

Key Findings

  • Responses diverge sharply by household type: single mothers show a significant negative labor-force participation response (β ≈ −0.14), while single fathers show a significant positive employment response (β ≈ +0.14) in higher-supply areas.
  • Full-time employment drops sharply at the CCAP subsidy-eligibility threshold (RD estimate ≈ −0.30, p = 0.001), while labor-force participation and employment show no comparable discontinuity.
  • The results suggest that how childcare assistance phases out may matter as much as the total amount of childcare available.
  • RD estimates are interpreted with bandwidth-sensitivity caveats, while placebo cutoffs show no comparable discontinuity.

Data: 20 years of Minnesota DHS/DCYF licensing records, ACS PUMS microdata (N ≈ 103,000), Minnesota DEED, QCEW, and FRED.

Methods: Difference-in-Differences / TWFE, HonestDiD, instrumental variables, and regression discontinuity at the CCAP threshold.

Software: R (fixest, rdrobust, ivreg, did).

Buffer Depletion and Price Instability

The inventory channel of monetary policy in U.S. manufacturing

Research Question

Do interest-rate shocks affect inventory behavior and producer-price volatility in inventory-intensive sectors?

Key Findings

  • After a contractionary monetary shock, inventory-intensive sectors show a significant and persistent increase in inventory-to-shipments ratios, peaking near +0.4 and remaining elevated for roughly 18 months.
  • The increase reflects shipments contracting faster than inventories can adjust.
  • Producer-price volatility rises more in inventory-intensive sectors and peaks about six months after the shock.
  • A 12-month placebo shock produces no comparable pattern, supporting a causal interpretation.

Data: Census M3/FRED, BLS Producer Price Index data through FRED, and Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco monetary-policy surprise measures.

Methods: Jordà local projections, two-way fixed effects, placebo tests, and split-sample robustness analysis.

Software: R with a fully reproducible analytical pipeline.

Working paper + published article

Oil Prices and Geopolitical Risk

What 35 years of data say about crude oil and geopolitical uncertainty

Research Question

Does geopolitical uncertainty move oil prices, or does it primarily affect oil-price volatility?

Key Findings

  • A two-state Markov-switching model identifies a distinct high-volatility crisis regime, with probabilities rising around the 1990 Gulf War, 2008, 2014–2016, the 2020 pandemic shock, and 2022.
  • Local projections show that a geopolitical-risk shock is followed by a modest cumulative decline in WTI returns of approximately 0.6% over the following month.
  • The evidence suggests geopolitical risk is expressed more strongly through volatility and regime switching than through persistent oil-price increases.
  • The project also evaluates WTI time-series momentum over one-, three-, six-, and twelve-month lookback windows.

Data: Caldara–Iacoviello Geopolitical Risk Index, WTI spot prices, and macroeconomic indicators, 1988–2026.

Methods: Markov switching, EGARCH, Jordà local projections, Newey–West inference, and time-series momentum analysis.

Software: R.

M.S. thesis — St. Cloud State University

Spillover Effects of U.S. Fiscal Policy on OECD Monetary Policy

How U.S. fiscal shocks transmit to central-bank decisions in more than 30 economies

Research Question

How do expansionary U.S. fiscal-policy shocks influence short-term policy rates across OECD economies?

Key Findings

  • Policy responses diverge systematically across countries.
  • High-income OECD economies tend to respond to a U.S. fiscal stimulus shock with expansionary monetary policy.
  • Other OECD economies are more likely to tighten monetary policy when facing the same shock.
  • The two-stage design first extracts U.S. structural shocks and then estimates each country's dynamic policy-rate response.

Data: OECD Statistics, FRED, and national-accounts datasets.

Methods: Cholesky-identified SVAR, ARDL, cointegration, impulse-response functions, and forecast-error variance decomposition.

Software: R and Stata.
